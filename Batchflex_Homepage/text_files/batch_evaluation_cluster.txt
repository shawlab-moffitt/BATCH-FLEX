<b>Cluster Plots</b>
<p><b>BatchFLEX</b> can calulate the optimal number of clusters using <b>Cluster Plots</b> which can found by clicking the second <b>Cluster Plots</b> tab after clicking the <b>"Step 2 - Batch Correction"</b> tab and the first <b>Cluster Plots</b> tab.</p>
<p>To help improve calculation speed, the <b>top 2000</b> most variable features by the <b>mean absolute deviation</b> are selected by default, however, the feature number and variance measure can be easily altered by the user. These parameters can be found under the <b>Cluster Parameters</b> section of the sidepanel under the <b>"Step 2 - Batch Correction"</b> tab after selecting the <b>Cluster Plots</b> tab. </p>
<p><b>BatchFLEX</b> offers multiple different methods of calculating the optimal cluster number, including the <b>Elbow Plot</b>, the <b>Silhouette Plot</b>, and the <b>Dunn Index Plot</b>. 
<p>The <b>elbow plot</b> shows the ideal cluster number at the <b>inflection point</b> of the line graph where the initial steep drop-off levels out to form the shape of an elbow. Many times, however, the <b>elbow plot</b> has no discernable inflection point and thus an ideal cluster number is difficult to interpret. To address this issue, BatchFLEX also calculates the ideal cluster number using a <b>silhouette plot</b> and the <b>dunn index plot</b>. These plots are more easily interpretable as the <b>point of the highest value</b> and is indicated on the plot using a <b>dashed line</b>.</p>

<p>Users can use these analyses to objectively determine whether correction improves the clustering of the data to better correlate with the variable of interest. The <b>Silhouette</b> plot in our example dataset shows that after <b>Limma</b> correction, the <b>ideal cluster number</b> is <b>two</b>, which correlates well with the broad <b>myeloid</b> and <b>lymphoid</b> lineages present in the dataset.</p>
